The "Cheers" system is being developed by "Wine Ha'Emek," a wine trading company, to manage key business operations, including inventory management, customer management, and order processing and delivery.

The sales staff are responsible for placing customer orders, with each order being assigned to a specific employee. Orders can be categorized as either regular or urgent and include the following details: order number, order date, current status (in process, dispatched, delivered, paid, suspended, or canceled), shipment date, and the assigned employee's information (ID, name, phone number, office address, email, and employment start date). The actual delivery of orders is managed by an external company, called "GalGalim," and falls outside the scope of the current system.

Orders may consist of multiple wines in different quantities. To minimize delivery costs, customers can place joint orders. In such cases, the system must track which wines and quantities have been ordered by each customer. For each customer in an order, the system records their ID, name, phone number, delivery address, email, and the date of first contact with the company. Company employees are allowed to order wines under the same conditions as regular customers. 

Urgent orders are processed with priority and must be placed by a single customer to ensure prompt delivery. When placing an urgent order, the priority level (a number between 1 and 5) and the expected delivery time must be specified. Regular orders, on the other hand, allow multiple customers to combine their wine selections into a single order to minimize delivery costs. In such cases, the system tracks the specific wines and quantities ordered by each customer, as well as the main customer who is responsible for the entire order.

For every customer included in an urgent or regular order, the system records their ID, name, phone number, delivery address, email, and the date of first contact with the company. Company employees may also place orders under the same conditions as regular customers.

Only employees in the marketing department are authorized to update wine information and types. All other employees will have view-only access. For example, sales staff can browse available wines when placing orders for customers. Each type of wine is identified by its name and serial number. Examples of wine types include red, white, sparkling, fortified, rosé, and dessert wines. Additional details for each wine type include food pairings; In addition to the name of the dish, up to 5 links (URLs) to possible recipes will be kept. Marketing employees can also recommend occasions suitable for drinking each wine type, providing a general description of the occasion (e.g., marriage proposal, nature hike, cheese night), along with the season and location (indoor/outdoor).

At the start of each month, the system will receive XML data from another system, called "Gefen," regarding wine producers and the wines they produce. Additionally, weekly updates will be provided on the quantity purchased (each wine is produced by a single manufacturer). For each manufacturer, the system will store the full name, contact phone number, address, and email. A unique automatic identifier is assigned to each new manufacturer. For each wine, the system will capture the name, description, production year, price per bottle, sweetness level (dry, semi-dry, semi-sweet, sweet), and product image. Wines are distinguished by their manufacturer number and catalog number. Marketing staff will link wines to their types and can modify price details and product images. Each wine is stored in a specific location (e.g., main refrigerator, cellar), and each storage location has a unique number and name. The system tracks the number of bottles of each wine at each storage location (wines may be stored in multiple locations and a single location may store multiple wines). As wines are sold only from available stock, inventory must be updated after each sale.

The "Cheers" system will also generate the following reports: (1) (1) Unproductive Employees Report: Generated by a sales employee, this report takes a time period as a parameter and displays the personal information of employees who placed fewer than 2 urgent orders OR fewer than 4 regular orders within that period; (2) Current Inventory Report by Wine: This report provides the inventory levels of all wines and is sent to the Gefen system in JSON format every weekend to assist with restocking preparations; (3) Wine Recommendation Report for Customers: Produced by a sales employee, this report returns a list of available wines that match the customer's preferences based on food, occasion, and wine type.

Recently, the need to more closely monitor orders and deliveries has arisen, leading to the decision that an order will be placed for a single customer, but multiple orders can be consolidated into one delivery.

When a "regular" order is placed, a notification is sent to the "GalGalim" company with the delivery address and the quantity of bottles in the order. If there is an existing delivery scheduled for that city that can accommodate the order, the order is added to that delivery. "GalGalim" operates under minimum and maximum bottle limits for shipments — orders will not be sent if they fall below the minimum, and an order cannot be included in a delivery if it exceeds the maximum. A delivery is dispatched when the bottle count exceeds the minimum, or at the explicit request of a "GalGalim" employee. For "urgent" orders, delivery is initiated immediately without delay.


During transit, issues may arise. If the delivery involves an urgent order, any problems will be resolved, and the delivery will continue to its final, successful destination. However, if the delivery is for "regular" orders, it will be cancelled and require the creation of a new delivery.
If a delivery for a particular order is not made within five days of the order being placed, the order enters a "clarification" status and remains there until the delivery is successfully dispatched (remember that deliveries can include multiple orders) or cancelled. After an order is dispatched for delivery but before it reaches the customer, payment must be made. If the delivery fails to arrive and the customer has already paid, a refund must be issued.

At any point, a customer can suspend their order, causing the corresponding delivery to be delayed. If the customer decides to resume the order, the processing of both the order and the delivery will continue from where they left off. Alternatively, the customer can cancel the order, in which case the order is cancelled but the delivery remains pending for additional orders.
